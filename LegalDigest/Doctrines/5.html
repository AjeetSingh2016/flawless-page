Introduction


Estoppel falls under Chapter 8 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 specifically defined under Section 115 of Indian Evidence Act, 1872 as, “When one person has, by his declaration, act or omission, intentionally caused or permitted another person to believe a thing to be true and to act upon such belief, neither he nor his representative shall be allowed, in any suit or proceeding between himself and such person or his representative, to deny the truth of that thing.”


The doctrine is based on the Latin maxims allegans contraria non audiendus est which means that a person alleging contradictory facts shall not be heard.


Ingredients of Section 115 -


  1. Where one person has by his statement, act or omission,


  2. intentionally, caused another person


  3. to believe a thing to be true and


  4. act on that belief,


  5. the former shall not be allowed to deny the truth of the statement, act or omission.


    We often see promises being made and later broken. While in some cases we can do nothing about it, but in certain circumstances, particularly in matters related to land or property, there is a possibility to bring a claim to enforce a broken promise. This is called proprietary

    estoppel. In Thorner v. Major it was laid down that in order to claim a right under proprietary estoppel these things have to be proved:


    • That representation has been made.


    • That the party believed it to be true and acted upon it.


    • That the party suffered a loss as a result of such representation.


No estoppel against the statue


There cannot be an estoppel to defeat the provisions of a valid law even if the representation was made by the government itself. As it is well said in Minister of National Revenue v Stickel (1972), "estoppel does not operate on the basis of a representation as to an interpretation of the law. Of course, a party can contend that the law itself was not validly made or was unconstitutional but when once it is shown that the law was validly made, no estoppel can be pleaded against the statute.

Is estoppel is applicable on Accused in criminal Matters


Munir says that as "estoppel is a rule of civil actions...it has no application in criminal proceedings". With respect, it is submitted that the question has to be viewed from two angles—as against (a) the accused and (b) against the prosecution. As in KM Nanavati v State of Maharashtra, 1962 , the accused is not estopped from pleading the defense of accident as well as of sudden and grave provocation. The accused can also retract a confession that he has made, and the Courts do not draw any adverse inference against the accused, and a retracted confession is, on other hand, treated as evidence though of a

weak kind. Thus, estoppel does not apply as against an accused in a criminal proceeding. Thus, estoppel does not apply as against an accused in a criminal proceeding.


Estoppel in case of Minor


In the landmark judgment of Mohori Bibee v Dharmo Das Ghose, (1903) , which held that a minor's contract was void, the Privy Council expressed no opinion on the question whether a minor could be estopped under section 115 of the Evidence Act, 1872.


The opposite view has been taken by Allahabad High Court in Jagarnath singh vs lalta prasad, 1909 that a minor is estopped from pleading that he is a minor even in cases relating to contractual capacity provided there is fraud on his part.


In Gadigappa vs Balan gowda,1931 view expressed by the Bombay High Court was that estoppel could be applied against a minor but in cases relating to his capacity to contract Section 11 of the Contract Act, 1872, will prevail over section 115 of the Evidence Act, 1872.


Promissory estoppel


The doctrine of promissory estoppel implies that when a person with the intention of making a lawful relationship makes a promise to another person and that other person acts upon the promise then that promise becomes an obligation on the person making it. He cannot deny such promise. He has to perform it without any neglect. In order to apply the doctrine it is not necessary that the promisee must suffer damage. It is enough that he has changed his dependence position on the assurance of the promise. Just like individuals the government can also enter into contracts with the individual and make promise to the individual. The government also cannot go back from the promise once made.

Conditions for Application of Doctrine


  1. There should be a promise from one person to another person.

  2. The promise should be made as such the other person believes it.

  3. The other person believing on it should have relied on that promise.

  4. By relying on the promise the other person should have acted upon it.

  5. The person relying should suffer loss or damage by such reliance.
