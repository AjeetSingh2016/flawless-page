Section 113A. Presumption as to abetment of suicide by a married woman - When the question is whether the commission of suicide by a woman had been abetted by her husband or any relative of her husband and it is shown that she had committed suicide within a period of seven years from the date of her marriage and that her husband or such relative of her husband had subjected her to cruelty, the court may presume, having regard to all the other circumstances of the case, that such suicide had been abetted by her husband or by such relative of her husband.


Explanation - For the purposes of this section, "cruelty" shall have the same meaning as in section 498A of the Indian Penal Code.


Section 113B Presumption as to dowry death - When the question is whether a person has committed the dowry death of a woman and it is shown that soon before her death such woman had been subjected by such person to cruelty or harassment for, or in connection with, any demand for dowry, the court shall presume that such person had caused the dowry death.


Explanation. For the purposes of this section, dowry death shall have the same meaning as in section 304B of the Indian Penal Code.


The Dowry Prohibition Act has been passed in 1961 making demanding and giving dowry punishable offences, the dowry related offences have been on the rise causing considerable concern for the law makers and enforcement agencies. The traditional principles of criminal justice based on the presumption of innocence of the accused and requirement of proof beyond all reasonable doubt which were the quintessence of the justice delivery system, proved to be the undoing of the system in tackling organized crimes and other special offences.

To meet the extraordinary situation, sections 113A and 113B were added to the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 and the sections impose "reverse onus of proof" on the accused (See Maya devi vs Haryana, 2015).


Ingredients of Section 113A of Act


  1. The question is whether the suicide by the woman was abetted by her husband or any relative of the husband;

  2. It is shown that she had committed suicide within seven years from the date of her marriage;

  3. That the husband or his relative has subjected her to cruelty; then

  4. The Court may presume having regard to all other circumstances of the case that such suicide had been abetted by her husband or by such relative of her husband.


Under the section 113B, it is first necessary to prove that such woman has been subjected by such person to cruelty or harassment and secondly, such cruelty should have been or in connection with any demand for dowry and thirdly that this must have been soon before her death. If these are proved, the court 'shall presume' the person caused the dowry death. Of course, the words 'shall presume' mean that the court is, in such circumstances, bound to presume that such person had caused the dowry death but still the presumption is rebuttable.


The need for insertion of section 113-B as also section 304B in the Penal Code has been stated in the 91st Report of the Law Commission (1983) on 'Dowry Deaths and Law Reform'. In Shamlal v. State of Haryana, 1997 , the Supreme Court had occasion to deal with section 113-B. It stated that it is imperative, for invoking the presumption under section 113-B, to prove that 'soon before her death' she was subjected to such cruelty or harassment.

Irrespective of the fact whether the accused has any direct connection with the death or not, he shall be presumed to have committed dowry death provided the ingredients of the section have been proved. Where the death of the wife was concurrently found to be unnatural viz. by strangulation, and there was demand for dowry and also there was cruelty on the part of the husband, the presumption under section 113B must be held to be rightly drawn (Hemchand v. State of Haryana, 1995).


Section 113-A, Evidence Act mandates that when a woman commits suicide within seven years of her marriage and it is shown that her husband or any relative of her husband had subjected her to cruelty as per the terms defined in Section 498-A IPC, the court may presume that such suicide has been abetted by the husband or the relative, Pinakin Mahipatray Rawal v. State of Gujarat, (2013) 10 SCC 48


It may be noted that the word "dowry" does not occur anywhere in section 498A either in the main part or in the explanation. Hence, the "wilful conduct" or the "harassment” (Heeralal vs State of Rajasthan,2017) coming under clauses (a) and (b) may or may not be related to dowry. Hence, "the definition of 'cruelty' is much wider and is not confined for dowry harassment.” (see State By Kamakshipalya Police v Maregowda, 2001)


Though section 498A refers to "any unlawful demand for any property or valuable security", it does not link it to dowry as does section 113B. Again, section 2 (2) of the Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961 defines "dowry" in similar terms as "any property or valuable security given or agreed to be given either directly or indirectly" but it says that it should be agreed to be given "as consideration for the marriage" and the latter link is missing in section 498A.


Note: ‘All the circumstances’ phrase employed under Section 113A of Evidence Act includes Suicide Notes and dying Declaration (See Ramesh Kumar v Chhattisgarh, 2001)

(In Jagdish Chander vs state of Haryana, 1988), it was held that the first requisite for raising the presumption under Section 113A, Evidence Act is that it must be proved that wife was subjected to cruelty as defined in Section 498A, I.P.C. and that the fact of wilful conduct of the accused in taking the liquor almost daily despite constant protest by his wife and his habit of coming home late in the night did not fulfil the essential ingredients of the cruelty as given in Section 498A, IPC.


As per State of West Bengal vs Orilal Jaiswal, 1994…The presumption under section 113A is of "may presume" variety and hence not a mandatory presumption, and the presumption depends on the Court's assessment "having regard to all the other circumstances of the case".


As per Dr Nageshwar Rao “In assessing the evidence in rebuttal of the presumption, the Court must have "regard to all the other circumstances of the case" as required under section 498A as to whether the "willful conduct" of the husband "is of such a nature as is likely to drive the woman to commit suicide."


How much cruelty is too much is case-specific and depends on factors such as: (1) the kind of conduct; (2) length of the conduct; (3) the sensitive nature of the woman, and (4) their relative social background.”


Position of Estoppel in Non Compoundable Offence


In some cases, the Courts have held that the compromise arrived at by the parties was unlawful as the offence is non-compoundable under section 320 Cr PC and the parties could


not be estopped from going back on the compromise In some other decisions, the Supreme Court and High Courts have quashed the criminal proceedings "to prevent abuse of the

process of any Court or otherwise to secure the ends of justice" under section 482 of Cr PC 1973.


The question was ultimately referred to a larger Bench of the Supreme Court in Gian Singh v Punjab, (2012), and the Supreme Court, upholding the decisions in BS Joshi, Nikhil Merchant and Manoj Sharma case to quash the criminal proceedings, held:


“Quashing of offence or criminal proceedings on the ground of settlement between an offender and victim is not the same thing as compounding of offence. They are different and not interchangeable.”


While compounding of an offence under section 320 is an act of the parties, quashing of proceedings under section 482 is an act of the Court, and the latter course is not violative of section 320.


Ingredients of Section 113B of Evidence act, 1872


(As per Apex court in Maharastra vs Rajendra, 2014)


  • When the question is whether a person has committed the dowry death of a woman


  • it is shown that soon before her death


  • such woman had been subjected by such person to cruelty or harassment for, or in

    connection with, any demand for dowry;


  • the court shall presume that such person had caused the dowry death.

    Distinction Between Section 113A and 113B


    1. Section 113B provides for mandatory presumption by employing the terms "the Court shall presume" whereas Section 113A creates a "may presume". (See Bansi lal vs Haryana, 2011)


    2. Section 113A is not linked to dowry harassment but section 113B is so expressly linked.


    3. Under section 113A, the woman must have committed suicide within seven years from the date of marriage to draw the presumption against her husband or his relative but under section 113B "soon before her death" the woman must have been subjected to dowry harassment or cruelty.


    4. Section 113B places a heavier burden on the accused than section 113A. (See Anand Kumar vs State of Madhya Pradesh, 2011).


Courts have held that the terms "relative of the husband" in a criminal statute like section 304B should be restrictively interpreted and the phrase refers only to a person related by blood, marriage or adoption…. (See U Suvetha vs State by Inspector of police,2009)


Why punishment under section 304b is lesser with repect to section 302 of IPC


Because under section 304b the Court proceeds on the basis of a presumption and not positive proof. It was held that (see Jasvinder Saini vs (Government of NCT Delhi), 2013):

“A charge under Section 304-B IPC is not a substitute for a charge of murder punishable under Section 302. As in the case of murder in every case under Section 304-B also there is a death involved. The question whether it is murder punishable under Section 302 IPC or a dowry death punishable under Section 304-B IPC depends upon the fact situation and the evidence in the case.”


And the acquittal under section 302 of IPC can never be a justification to rebuttable presumption under section 113B


In Vijay Pal singh vs Uttrakhand, 2002 even if an accused is acquitted under Section 302 of IPC, if there is evidence available so as to satisfy the ingredients of Section 304B of IPC, the accused can still be convicted under Section 304B of IPC, in case there is no rebuttal of presumption on the death as caused by the accused.
