Introduction


Most of the appropriations made by Parliament are on an annual basis. The Executive thus comes before Parliament every year to ask for grants for the ensuing year so that Parliament gets an opportunity of reviewing, criticising and discussing the activities and policies pursued by the Government during the preceding year. The parliamentary process to make annual appropriations passes through several stages. The first stage is the presentation of an annual financial statement, popularly known as the Budget. The Budget is presented by the Finance Minister on the last working day in February every year. It is the statement of the estimated receipts and expenditure of the Government of India for the following year (April 1 to March 31).


“Annual financial statement” is defined under Article 112 as a statement of the estimated receipts and expenditure of the Government of India for that year, and President laid down the bill before the parliament. And as per Article 112(2) estimates of expenditure embodied in the annual financial statement broadly divided into two aspects –

  1. The sums required to meet expenditure described by the Condition as expenditure charged upon the Consolidated Fund of India; and (See Art.112(2)(a))

  2. The sums required to meet other expenditure proposed to be made from the Consolidated Fund of India, and shall distinguish expenditure on revenue account from other expenditure. (see Art. 112(2)(b)).


    Article 112(3) deals with expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund of India. Like salaries and allowances of the Chairman and the Deputy Chairman of the Council of States and the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker of the House of the People (See Art. 112(3)(b)), the salaries, allowances and pensions payable to or in respect of Judges of the Supreme Court (See Art. 112(2)(d)(i)). And parliament by law declare any other expenditure to be charged on the Consolidated Fund of India (See Art.112(3)(g)).

    As per Article 113(1), The estimates of expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund of India are open to discussion but not to a vote in Parliament and As per Article 113(2), Estimates relates to other expenditure shall be submitted in the form of demands for grants to the House of the People, and the House of the People shall have power to assent, or to refuse to assent and No demand for a grant shall be made except on the recommendation of the President. (See Article 113(3)).


    A member has, therefore, very limited opportunity to distort the Budget. He can only move cut motions to reduce the amount of a demand and through such motions he may criticise the Government


    Cut Motions - The motions to reduce the amounts of demands for grants are called ‘Cut Motions’. The object of a cut motion is to draw the attention of the House to the matter specified therein.


    Cut Motions can be classified into three categories:—


    1. Disapproval of Policy Cut;

    2. Economy Cut; and

    3. Token Cut.


Disapproval of Policy Cut


A cut motion which says “That the amount of the demand be reduced to Re. 1” implies that the mover disapproves of the policy underlying the demand. The member giving notice of such a Cut Motion has to indicate in precise terms the particulars of the policy which he proposes to discuss. Discussion is confined to the specific point or points mentioned in the notice and it is open to the member to advocate an alternative policy.

Economy Cut


Where the object of the motion is to effect economy in the expenditure, the form of the

motion is “That the amount of the demand be reduced by Rs...(a specified amount)”. The amount suggested for reduction may be either a lump-sum reduction in the demand or omission or reduction of an item in the demand.


Token Cut


Where the object of the motion is to ventilate a specific grievance within the sphere of responsibility of the Government of India, its form is: “That the amount of the demand be reduced by Rs. 100”.


To appropriate amount from consolidated fund of India through Appropriation Act


As per Article 114(1), the grants under article 113 have been made by the House of the People, there shall be introduced a Bill to provide for the appropriation out of the Consolidated Fund of India. As per Article 114(1)(b), The Appropriation Bill provides for appropriation out of the Consolidated Fund of all moneys required to meet the grants assented to by Lok Sabha and the expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund, and in no case the amount under each head can exceed what was shown previously in the Budget.


As per Article 114(2), No amendment can be proposed to this Bill in any House of Parliament so as to vary the amount or alter the destination of any grant previously agreed to by Lok Sabha or to vary the amount of an expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund. The decision of the presiding officer as to whether an amendment is admissible or not under this clause is final.

As per Article 116(1)(b), Notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Chapter, the House of the People shall have power to make a grant for meeting an unexpected demand upon the resources of India when on account of the magnitude or indefinite character of the service, the demand cannot be stated with usual details. Votes of credit may be used in times of emergency when Parliament may vote a lump sum without allocating it to any particular object.


Lok Sabha is also authorised to make an exceptional grant which forms no part of the current service of any financial year [Art. 116(1)(c)]. After the Lok Sabha assents to any of these grants, Parliament has to enact a law to withdraw moneys from the Consolidated Fund. In making these grants and passing the law, the procedure prescribed for making annual appropriations is to be followed [Art. 116(2)].


As per Article 114(3), no money shall be withdrawn under Article 114, 115 from the Consolidated Fund of India except under appropriation made by law passed in accordance with the provisions of this article.
